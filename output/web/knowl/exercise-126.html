Exercise 1.

Determine if the argument is valid or invalid. If it is valid, state whether it is an example of modus ponens or modus tollens. If it is invalid, state whether it it exhibits converse error or inverse error.
  1. All healthy people eat an apple a day.
    Keisha eats an apple a day.
    \(\therefore\) Keisha is a healthy person.
  2. If the product of two numbers is 0, then at least one of the numbers is 0.
    For a particular number \(x\text{,}\) neither \((2x+1)\) nor \((x-7)\) equals 0.
    \(\therefore\) The product \((2x+1)(x-7)\) is not 0.
  3. All honest people pay their taxes.
    Darth is not honest.
    \(\therefore\) Darth does not pay his taxes.
  4. Any sum of two rational numbers is rational.
    The sum \(r+s\) is rational.
    \(\therefore\) The numbers \(r\) and \(s\) are both rational.
  5. If an infinite series converges, then its terms go to 0.
    The terms of the infinite series \(\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}\frac{1}{n}\) go to 0.
    \(\therefore\) The infinite series \(\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}\frac{1}{n}\) converges.
  6. If an infinite series converges, then its terms go to 0.
    The terms of the infinite series \(\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}\frac{n}{n+1}\) do not go to 0.
    \(\therefore\) The infinite series \(\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}\frac{n}{n+1}\) does not converge.
